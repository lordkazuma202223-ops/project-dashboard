## Debate Log

### Round 1: Initial Positions

---

**Pragmatist:**
Let's cut to the chase. Realistically, you're looking at two paths with different ROI timelines.

If you go to SIT: You're committing 2-4 more years and roughly SGD 30,000-40,000 per year in tuition as an international student. That's SGD 60,000-160,000 opportunity cost. What's the payback? SIT degrees are practical, but entry-level salaries after graduation might be SGD 3,000-3,500/month. You're looking at 4-7 years just to break even on your education investment.

If you start working now: You immediately earn SGD 2,200-2,800/month fresh from polytechnic. In 4 years, you'd have earned SGD 130,000+ and gained 4 years of work experience. That experience often matters more than the degree after your first job.

At the end of the day, the question is: Does SIT give you enough salary premium to justify the cost? For many fields, the answer is "not immediately." Work experience pays off faster.

My starting position: Lean toward working first, unless SIT opens a door that's completely closed to you without it.

---

**Empath:**
I hear Pragmatist's numbers, and they matter. But let's look at how this decision affects you as a person.

You're an international student - that means pressure from family back home, perhaps expectations of "making it" in Singapore. Working immediately might feel like giving up on your academic dreams. It might weigh on you emotionally, wondering "what if I had studied more?"

On the flip side, entering university when you're not academically ready or motivated can be draining. If SIT feels like a "consolation prize" compared to NTU/NUS, that bitterness can affect your mental health and performance.

Let's consider: How do YOU feel about studying versus working? What brings you more peace and excitement right now? The money matters, but so does your emotional wellbeing. A decision that aligns with your heart will carry you through harder times.

My starting position: I want to understand what YOU truly want, beyond the numbers and expectations. If university excites you, we can find a way. If work feels right, that's valid too.

---

**Analyst:**
Let's examine the evidence systematically.

Key variables we need data on:
1. **Salary differentials:** Poly diploma vs SIT degree - studies show degree holders earn 15-25% more long-term in Singapore, BUT the first 2-4 years show minimal difference. The premium materializes mid-career.

2. **Field-specific factors:** In tech/engineering, skills and portfolio often outweigh degrees. In finance/accounting, degrees (and professional certifications) matter significantly more. What's your field?

3. **Employment statistics:** 2023 data shows ~85% of SIT graduates employed within 6 months. For poly graduates in Singapore, employment rate is similar (~90% in related fields). The degree doesn't dramatically change employment odds.

4. **International student considerations:** After graduation, work pass eligibility depends on salary thresholds. A degree holder starting at SGD 3,500+ has better odds than diploma holder at SGD 2,400+. This is a crucial data point.

Logically speaking, the key question isn't immediate earnings - it's whether the degree improves your employment eligibility and long-term ceiling.

My starting position: Neutral - more data needed on your specific field and career goals. The evidence points to "it depends."

**Vision:**
Imagine a world where we stopped thinking in 4-year ROI windows and started thinking in 40-year careers.

What could SIT unlock for you? The degree isn't just about entry salary - it's about doors that open 5, 10, 15 years from now. Management roles often require degrees. International mobility sometimes requires degree equivalence. Some professional certifications (like CPA) require degree prerequisites.

But also imagine another path: Starting work now, gaining real-world experience, earning money, possibly pursuing part-time or executive education later. Many successful leaders took non-linear paths. Work experience + degree later can be a powerful combination.

The future belongs to those who adapt, not just those who follow the standard timeline. If SIT excites you - go! The learning, the network, the growth environment could transform your trajectory. If work calls you - answer it! Experience is its own education.

We have an opportunity to reimagine what "success" looks like beyond conventional metrics.

My starting position: Both paths can lead to extraordinary futures. Choose the one that sparks your curiosity and growth.

---

**Skeptic:**
I see a lot of optimism here, but what are we missing?

Have we considered:
1. **Student loan risk:** As an international student, you can't use Singapore government loans. Private loans have higher interest. What if you can't find a job after graduation? The debt doesn't disappear.

2. **SIT's reputation vs NTU/NUS:** You mentioned your GPA isn't high enough for top schools - that suggests academic performance has been challenging. Will SIT's curriculum be a better fit, or will you struggle again? Failing after investing time and money would be worse than not trying.

3. **Immigration uncertainty:** Singapore's work pass rules change. Degrees help, but nothing is guaranteed. In 4 years, the landscape could shift - for or against you.

4. **Opportunity cost of time:** You're already at poly, presumably 18-19 years old. By 23 with a degree, you're competing against 23-year-olds who've been working since 19. Experience sometimes beats credentials.

5. **Market saturation:** More people getting degrees means degree inflation. Is SIT still valuable enough to justify the cost?

The risk is you invest 4 years and SGD 100,000+ and end up in the same job you could have had today - but with debt and 4 years of lost wages.

My starting position: The default should be working first. Only choose SIT if you can clearly articulate why it's worth the substantial risk.

Or start SIT if your field truly requires the degree for advancement. The right path depends on YOUR answers to: What field? What are your career goals? What's your financial situation?

**Analyst (responding to Pragmatist):**
Pragmatist, I hear your ROI calculation, but I think you're underestimating the non-linear value of education.

Your model assumes: (Degree Salary - Diploma Salary) × Years of Work = Educational Value

But this misses:
1. **Salary acceleration:** Degree holders often hit promotion milestones faster. After 5 years, the gap widens beyond 15-25%.

2. **Career switching flexibility:** A degree enables career pivots that a diploma might restrict. Data from Ministry of Manpower shows degree holders switch industries 2-3× more frequently with successful transitions.

3. **Geographic mobility:** If you ever want to work outside Singapore, a bachelor's degree is often a minimum requirement for work visas in other countries. That option value isn't in your spreadsheet.

4. **Network effects:** SIT's industry partnerships and alumni network - can you quantify the value of connections that might lead to opportunities years later?

The data doesn't support a simple "work first" conclusion. It supports "it depends on field and aspirations."

What's the field, specifically? That's the missing variable that shifts the equation decisively.
